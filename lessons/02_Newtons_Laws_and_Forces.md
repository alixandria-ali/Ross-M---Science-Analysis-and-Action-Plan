## Key Concepts to Master

### Newton's First Law (Law of Inertia)
An object at rest stays at rest, and an object in motion stays in motion at constant velocity, UNLESS acted upon by an unbalanced force.

### Newton's Second Law (F = ma)
**Force = mass × acceleration**

**CRITICAL UNDERSTANDING:**
- Same force, MORE mass → LESS acceleration
- Same force, LESS mass → MORE acceleration
- Same mass, MORE force → MORE acceleration

### Newton's Third Law (Action-Reaction)
For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction.

**CRITICAL UNDERSTANDING:**
- Forces ALWAYS come in pairs
- The forces are EQUAL in magnitude
- The forces are OPPOSITE in direction
- They act on DIFFERENT objects

## Common Mistakes to Avoid ⚠️

### ❌ WRONG: "In a collision, the car exerts more force than the wall"
### ✅ CORRECT: "Both exert EQUAL and opposite forces on each other"

### ❌ WRONG: "More mass means more acceleration with the same force"
### ✅ CORRECT: "More mass means LESS acceleration with the same force"

### ❌ WRONG: "The heavier object in a collision experiences more force"
### ✅ CORRECT: "Both objects experience the SAME force (Newton's 3rd Law)"

## Understanding F = ma

**Formula:** Force = mass × acceleration
- **Rearranged:** acceleration = Force ÷ mass (a = F/m)

**Key Insight:** If mass DOUBLES, acceleration is CUT IN HALF (with the same force)

**Example:**
- Object A: mass = 2 kg, force = 10 N → acceleration = 10/2 = 5 m/s²
- Object B: mass = 4 kg, force = 10 N → acceleration = 10/4 = 2.5 m/s²

Object B has twice the mass, so it accelerates at HALF the rate!

## Practice Questions

### Question 1
Two objects are pushed with the same force. Object A has twice the mass of Object B. Which statement is correct?

A. Object A accelerates more
B. Object B accelerates more
C. Both accelerate equally
D. Neither accelerates

<details>
<summary>Click to see answer</summary>

**Answer: B**

**Explanation:** Using F = ma, we can rearrange to a = F/m. If Object A has twice the mass but the same force, its acceleration is HALF that of Object B. More mass = less acceleration with the same force. Think of it like pushing a car vs. pushing a bicycle with the same force - the bicycle accelerates much more because it has less mass.

</details>

### Question 2
A car collides with a solid wall. Which statement is correct about the forces?

A. The car exerts greater force than the wall
B. The wall exerts greater force than the car
C. Both exert equal and opposite forces
D. Only the car exerts force

<details>
<summary>Click to see answer</summary>

**Answer: C**

**Explanation:** This is Newton's Third Law. When the car hits the wall, the car exerts a force on the wall, and the wall exerts an EQUAL and OPPOSITE force back on the car. The forces are always equal in magnitude during the collision. The car crumples because it's less rigid than the wall, but the forces are equal.

</details>

### Question 3
A tennis racket hits a ball. Why does the racket feel a force?

A. Newton's Third Law - the ball pushes back on the racket
B. Newton's First Law - objects resist change
C. Newton's Second Law - force creates acceleration
D. The Law of Gravity

<details>
<summary>Click to see answer</summary>

**Answer: A**

**Explanation:** Newton's Third Law states that for every action there's an equal and opposite reaction. When the racket exerts a force on the ball (action), the ball simultaneously exerts an equal force back on the racket (reaction). That's why you feel the impact in your hand.

</details>

### Question 4
A bowling ball (6 kg) and a tennis ball (0.06 kg) are dropped from the same height (ignoring air resistance). Which hits the ground with more force?

A. Bowling ball
B. Tennis ball
C. Same force
D. Neither hits with force

<details>
<summary>Click to see answer</summary>

**Answer: A**

**Explanation:** Although both balls accelerate at the same rate due to gravity (9.8 m/s²), the bowling ball has 100 times more mass. Using F = ma, with the same acceleration but 100x the mass, the bowling ball exerts 100x more force when it hits the ground. F = ma → more mass, same acceleration = more force.

</details>

### Question 5
If you push on a wall with 50 N of force, how much force does the wall push back on you?

A. 0 N (walls don't push)
B. 25 N (half the force)
C. 50 N (equal force)
D. 100 N (double the force)

<details>
<summary>Click to see answer</summary>

**Answer: C**

**Explanation:** Newton's Third Law - forces always come in equal and opposite pairs. If you push on the wall with 50 N, the wall pushes back on you with exactly 50 N. You don't go through the wall because it's pushing back on you with equal force!

</details>

### Question 6
A constant horizontal force is applied to a bowling ball sitting on a smooth table. What happens?

A. The ball remains motionless because it's heavy
B. The ball accelerates in the direction of the force
C. The ball moves at constant velocity
D. The ball accelerates then stops

<details>
<summary>Click to see answer</summary>

**Answer: B**

**Explanation:** According to Newton's Second Law (F = ma), when a constant force is applied to an object, it produces constant acceleration. The ball will accelerate (speed up) in the direction of the force. If the force continues, the acceleration continues. The ball is heavy, so it will accelerate slowly, but it WILL accelerate.

</details>

### Question 7
Two students push on each other while wearing roller skates. Student A (40 kg) and Student B (60 kg) push off with equal force. What happens?

A. Student A accelerates more
B. Student B accelerates more
C. Both accelerate equally
D. Neither moves

<details>
<summary>Click to see answer</summary>

**Answer: A**

**Explanation:** Newton's Third Law means they both experience the same force (equal and opposite). But Newton's Second Law (a = F/m) tells us that with the same force, the lighter person (Student A) will accelerate MORE than the heavier person (Student B). Student A has less mass, so Student A accelerates more and rolls back faster.

</details>

### Question 8
A rocket in space fires its engines. What makes the rocket move forward?

A. The rocket pushes against space
B. The engines push the rocket
C. Exhaust gases push backward, so rocket moves forward (Newton's 3rd Law)
D. Gravity pulls the rocket

<details>
<summary>Click to see answer</summary>

**Answer: C**

**Explanation:** This is a perfect example of Newton's Third Law. The rocket engines push exhaust gases backward (action force), and the gases push the rocket forward (reaction force). The forces are equal and opposite. The rocket doesn't need to "push against" anything - the action-reaction pair with the expelled gases is enough.

</details>

### Question 9
Which change would cause the GREATEST increase in acceleration (assuming force stays constant)?

A. Doubling the mass
B. Tripling the mass
C. Halving the mass
D. Adding a small amount of mass

<details>
<summary>Click to see answer</summary>

**Answer: C**

**Explanation:** From a = F/m, we see that acceleration is inversely proportional to mass. Halving the mass DOUBLES the acceleration (with constant force). Doubling the mass HALVES the acceleration. Halving produces the greatest increase.

Example: If F = 10 N and m = 4 kg → a = 2.5 m/s²
If we halve mass to 2 kg → a = 5 m/s² (doubled!)

</details>

### Question 10
A 2 kg book sits on a table. Gravity pulls it down with 20 N. Why doesn't it fall through the table?

A. The book is too heavy
B. The table pushes up with 20 N (Newton's 3rd Law)
C. Gravity stops working
D. The book pushes itself up

<details>
<summary>Click to see answer</summary>

**Answer: B**

**Explanation:** The book exerts 20 N downward on the table (due to gravity). According to Newton's Third Law, the table exerts an equal 20 N upward on the book. These equal and opposite forces balance out, so the book remains at rest. This upward force from the table is called the "normal force."

</details>

## Quick Reference Card 📋

**Newton's Laws Summary:**

**1st Law:** Objects resist changes in motion (inertia)

**2nd Law:** F = ma
- More mass → Less acceleration (same force)
- More force → More acceleration (same mass)

**3rd Law:** Equal and opposite forces
- Forces come in pairs
- Same magnitude, opposite direction
- Act on different objects

**Key Formula:**
- F = ma
- a = F/m (acceleration equals force divided by mass)

---

## Real-World Applications

**Seatbelts (1st Law):** Your body wants to keep moving when the car stops suddenly

**Pushing a shopping cart (2nd Law):** Empty cart accelerates faster than full cart

**Walking (3rd Law):** Your foot pushes backward on ground, ground pushes you forward

**Rocket launch (3rd Law):** Exhaust pushes down, rocket pushes up
