## Key Concepts to Master

### The Fundamental Rule of Charges

**LIKE charges REPEL, OPPOSITE charges ATTRACT**

```
+ and + = REPEL (push apart)  ❌ ← →
- and - = REPEL (push apart)  ❌ ← →
+ and - = ATTRACT (pull together) ✓ → ←
```

Think of magnets: Same poles repel, opposite poles attract!

---

## Understanding Electric Charge

### Two Types of Charge:
- **Positive (+):** Has lost electrons
- **Negative (-):** Has gained extra electrons

### Neutral Objects:
- Equal number of positive and negative charges
- Net charge = zero

### How Objects Become Charged:
- **Friction (rubbing):** Electrons transfer from one object to another
- Example: Rubbing a balloon on your hair transfers electrons to the balloon
- The balloon becomes negative (gained electrons)
- Your hair becomes positive (lost electrons)

## Common Mistakes to Avoid ⚠️

### ❌ WRONG: "Two balloons with the same charge attract each other"
### ✅ CORRECT: "Two balloons with the same charge REPEL each other"

### ❌ WRONG: "Opposite charges repel"
### ✅ CORRECT: "Opposite charges ATTRACT"

### ❌ WRONG: "Rubbing removes charge from both objects"
### ✅ CORRECT: "Rubbing transfers electrons, making one positive and one negative"

## Practice Questions

### Question 1
Two balloons are rubbed with wool. Both become negatively charged. What happens when they are brought near each other?

A. They attract (pull together)
B. They repel (push apart)
C. Nothing happens
D. They lose their charge

<details>
<summary>Click to see answer</summary>

**Answer: B**

**Explanation:** Both balloons have the SAME charge (both negative). LIKE charges REPEL. The two negative charges push against each other, causing the balloons to move apart. Remember: Same charges repel (- and - push apart, + and + push apart).

</details>

### Question 2
A positively charged rod is brought near a negatively charged object. What happens?

A. They repel each other
B. They attract each other
C. Both become neutral
D. Nothing happens

<details>
<summary>Click to see answer</summary>

**Answer: B**

**Explanation:** OPPOSITE charges ATTRACT. Positive (+) and negative (-) are opposite charges, so they pull toward each other. This is why dust (slightly charged) sticks to TV screens, and why you can pick up paper with a charged balloon.

</details>

### Question 3
You rub a balloon on your hair. The balloon becomes negatively charged. What happened to the electrons?

A. Electrons moved from hair to balloon
B. Electrons moved from balloon to hair
C. Electrons disappeared
D. Electrons were created in the balloon

<details>
<summary>Click to see answer</summary>

**Answer: A**

**Explanation:** When the balloon becomes negatively charged, it means the balloon GAINED electrons (negative charge = extra electrons). These electrons came FROM your hair. Your hair lost electrons and became positively charged, which is why your hair stands up - it's attracted to the negatively charged balloon!

Electrons move from hair → balloon
Hair: positive (lost electrons)
Balloon: negative (gained electrons)

</details>

### Question 4
Why do your hair strands stand up after rubbing a balloon on your head?

A. Your hairs are all negatively charged and repel each other
B. Your hairs are all positively charged and repel each other
C. Your hair is attracted to gravity
D. The balloon pulls your hair down

<details>
<summary>Click to see answer</summary>

**Answer: B**

**Explanation:** When you rub the balloon on your hair, electrons transfer from hair to balloon. Your hair strands all lose electrons and become POSITIVELY charged. Since all hair strands have the SAME (positive) charge, they REPEL each other and stand apart. LIKE charges repel!

</details>

### Question 5
An electroscope (device that detects charge) has two metal leaves that hang down. When a charged object touches it, the leaves spread apart. Why?

A. Both leaves get the same charge and repel
B. Leaves get opposite charges and attract
C. Leaves become magnetic
D. Air pushes them apart

<details>
<summary>Click to see answer</summary>

**Answer: A**

**Explanation:** When the charged object touches the electroscope, charge spreads to both leaves. Both leaves get the SAME type of charge (both positive OR both negative). Since they have the same charge, they REPEL each other and spread apart. This is a classic demonstration of like charges repelling!

</details>

### Question 6
Which pair of charges would have the STRONGEST attractive force?

A. Both slightly positive
B. One very positive, one very negative
C. Both slightly negative
D. One neutral, one positive

<details>
<summary>Click to see answer</summary>

**Answer: B**

**Explanation:** Opposite charges attract. The STRONGER the charges (more positive and more negative), the STRONGER the attraction between them. A very positive charge and a very negative charge would have the strongest attractive force. Neutral objects have no charge, so they don't experience electric attraction/repulsion.

</details>

### Question 7
Lightning is a large discharge of static electricity. Before lightning strikes, clouds become negatively charged at the bottom and the ground below becomes positively charged. What happens?

A. Nothing - opposite charges don't interact
B. Opposite charges attract, causing electrons to jump (lightning)
C. Same charges repel, preventing lightning
D. The charges cancel out

<details>
<summary>Click to see answer</summary>

**Answer: B**

**Explanation:** The negative bottom of the cloud and positive ground are OPPOSITE charges, so they ATTRACT strongly. When the attraction is strong enough, electrons jump from the cloud to the ground (or positive charges jump up) creating a lightning bolt. This is a massive discharge of static electricity caused by opposite charges attracting.

</details>

### Question 8
Three plastic rods are hanging from strings. Rod A and Rod B repel each other. Rod B and Rod C attract each other. If Rod A is positively charged, what are the charges on Rods B and C?

A. B is positive, C is negative
B. B is negative, C is positive
C. Both B and C are positive
D. Both B and C are negative

<details>
<summary>Click to see answer</summary>

**Answer: A**

**Explanation:**
- A and B repel → same charge → both positive
- B and C attract → opposite charges → if B is positive, C must be negative

Summary:
- Rod A: Positive (given)
- Rod B: Positive (repels A, so same charge)
- Rod C: Negative (attracts B, so opposite charge)

</details>

### Question 9
Why does a charged comb attract small pieces of paper?

A. Paper is magnetic
B. Charged comb induces opposite charge on paper, causing attraction
C. Paper has the same charge as the comb
D. Paper gains electrons

<details>
<summary>Click to see answer</summary>

**Answer: B**

**Explanation:** The charged comb creates an "induced charge" on the neutral paper. If the comb is negative, it pushes electrons in the paper to the far side, making the near side slightly positive. Opposite charges attract, so the paper is attracted to the comb. This is called "charging by induction" and is why static electricity can attract neutral objects!

</details>

### Question 10
What is the net charge of an atom with 10 protons and 10 electrons?

A. Positive
B. Negative
C. Neutral
D. Cannot determine

<details>
<summary>Click to see answer</summary>

**Answer: C**

**Explanation:** Protons are positive (+), electrons are negative (-). If the number of protons equals the number of electrons, the charges balance out:
- 10 positive charges + 10 negative charges = 0 (neutral)

An atom is neutral when it has equal numbers of protons and electrons. If it loses electrons, it becomes positive (more protons than electrons). If it gains electrons, it becomes negative (more electrons than protons).

</details>

## Quick Reference Card 📋

**THE ONE RULE TO REMEMBER:**
- **LIKE charges REPEL** (+ and +, or - and -)
- **OPPOSITE charges ATTRACT** (+ and -)

**Charge Basics:**
- **Positive (+)** = Lost electrons (fewer electrons than protons)
- **Negative (-)** = Gained electrons (more electrons than protons)
- **Neutral** = Equal protons and electrons

**How Charging Works:**
- **Friction/Rubbing:** Electrons transfer from one object to another
- **Conduction:** Direct contact transfers charge
- **Induction:** Charged object causes charge separation in nearby object

**Remember:**
- Electrons are NEGATIVE
- Protons are POSITIVE
- Only ELECTRONS move (protons stay in nucleus)

## Real-World Applications

**Static Electricity Examples:**
- Rubbing balloon on hair
- Clothes sticking together from dryer (static cling)
- Shock when touching doorknob after walking on carpet
- Lightning (massive static discharge)

**Useful Applications:**
- Photocopiers use static electricity to attract toner to paper
- Paint sprayers charge paint particles to stick to surfaces
- Air filters use static to trap dust particles
- Electrostatic precipitators clean smoke from factories
